01 — The issue

A small task, repeated thousands of times.

An organization decided to bring a field operation in-house instead of outsourcing it. The financial logic was sound — except for one cost hiding in the paperwork.

Every job required permits from local authorities. Across the territory, 24 different administrations each had their own rules: different forms, platforms, deadlines, contacts, and fees. For every single job, an employee had to identify the right procedure, gather documents, draw a compliant plan, and route it to the right people.

Done by hand, this consumed over 90 minutes per job — slow, error-prone, and entirely dependent on one person holding a maze of procedures in their head. The administrative overhead alone threatened to make the whole project unprofitable.

The hard part was never the work itself. It was the paperwork around it — and there was a mountain of it.

02 — The solution

One form. Every rule. Zero friction.

The fix was a single web tool that absorbs the complexity behind one interface. The employee fills out one form, regardless of location. The tool detects which administration applies, loads its specific rules, and produces everything required — automatically.

I built it in roughly 10 days, working with Claude. I brought the domain knowledge — researching every procedure, mapping every rule. Claude turned that knowledge into working software at a speed no individual could match alone.

03 — The impact

From 90 minutes to eleven.

04 — What transfers

The method, not the tool.

Forget the specifics. The reason this worked is a repeatable pattern that applies to almost any organization with a tedious, recurring process. Six principles generalize.

01

Target expensive friction

The biggest wins come from killing boring, repetitive work — not clever features. Find the task everyone dreads.

02

Human expertise is the moat

AI writes the code, but knowing what to build — the domain, the rules, the relationships — is irreplaceably human.

03

Turn knowledge into a system

When procedures live in a tool instead of someone's memory, you get an asset: easier onboarding, fewer errors, full traceability.

04

Price impact in money

"Saves time" is forgettable. "Saves €597K over ten years" gets approved. Translate minutes into euros, conservatively.

05

Design for the long run

The build is a day; operation is years. Separate data from logic so a non-developer can maintain it.

06

Co-construct for adoption

Tools fail on adoption, not technology. Build with the team, not for them, and measure the realized return.
